## Pre-flight Checklist

- [ ] Identify your motherboard or laptop manufacturer and model
- [ ] Have access to a second device for looking up documentation
- [ ] Ensure stable power supply (never interrupt a BIOS operation)
- [ ] If on a laptop, plug in the charger — do not run on battery

> **FIRMWARE WARNING**: Modifying BIOS/UEFI settings incorrectly can prevent your computer from booting. Interrupting a firmware update can permanently brick your device. Always ensure stable power and follow manufacturer instructions exactly.

## When to Reset BIOS/UEFI

Common symptoms that a BIOS reset can fix:

- Computer won't boot after changing settings
- Boot order is wrong and the OS won't load
- Overclocking settings causing instability
- Secure Boot preventing a valid OS from loading
- Hardware not detected after a change

## Method 1: Reset via UEFI Menu

1. Restart and enter UEFI settings (usually F2, Del, or F10 at the splash screen)
2. Navigate to **Exit** or **Save & Exit** tab
3. Select **"Load Optimized Defaults"** or **"Reset to Default"**
4. Save and exit
5. Your system should boot normally with factory BIOS settings

## Method 2: CMOS Battery Reset

If you cannot access the UEFI menu:

1. Power off the computer and unplug it
2. Open the case (desktop) or bottom panel (laptop)
3. Locate the CMOS battery (a silver coin-cell battery on the motherboard)
4. Remove the battery carefully
5. Wait 30 seconds
6. Reinsert the battery
7. Close the case and power on

> **ELECTRICAL SAFETY**: Always unplug the computer before opening the case. Discharge static by touching the metal case before handling components.

## Method 3: BIOS Recovery Jumper

Some motherboards have a CMOS reset jumper:

1. Power off and unplug
2. Locate the CLRTC or CMOS_RST jumper (check your motherboard manual)
3. Move the jumper from its default position to the reset position
4. Wait 10 seconds
5. Move the jumper back
6. Power on

## Restoring Boot Order

After a reset, you may need to reconfigure:

1. Enter UEFI settings
2. Go to the Boot tab
3. Set your OS drive as the first boot device
4. Enable or disable Secure Boot as needed
5. Save and exit

## Common Mistakes

- Interrupting a BIOS update (can permanently brick the board)
- Resetting BIOS on classroom or managed machines without IT approval
- Forgetting to restore boot order after reset
- Not grounding yourself before touching internal components

## Verification

- [ ] Computer boots to the operating system
- [ ] UEFI settings are accessible at startup
- [ ] Boot order is correct
- [ ] Date and time are correct (CMOS reset may reset the clock)
- [ ] All hardware is detected in the OS
